    Education and Early Career

    Shou Zi Chew was born and raised in Singapore, where he currently lives with his wife, Vivian Kao, and their two children. In 2006, he graduated from University College London with a degree in Economics.

    After graduation, he worked at Goldman Sachs for two years before moving to the United States to pursue a master’s degree at Harvard Business School.

    While studying at Harvard, Chew had a two-year internship with Facebook. During his time there, he gained valuable experience and developed a keen interest in the world of technology and social media.

    Career in Venture Capital

    After completing his MBA, Chew joined venture capital firm DST Global as a partner.

    In 2015, Chew left DST Global to join Xiaomi, a Chinese smartphone company. During his five-year tenure at the company, he served as the Chief Financial Officer and helped the company grow and expand into new markets.

    Appointment as TikTok CEO

    In March 2021, Shou Zi Chew was appointed as the new CEO of TikTok, replacing Kevin Mayer, a former Disney executive. As CEO, Chew reports directly to Liang Rubo, the CEO of ByteDance.

    TikTok Faces Intense Questioning Before U.S. Lawmakers

    TikTok, the popular video-sharing app, is currently facing intense scrutiny from U.S. lawmakers over its consumer privacy and data security practices.

    The U.S. House Committee on Energy and Commerce is conducting a hearing on the matter, as the possibility of a TikTok ban in the United States gains momentum in Washington.

    According to the committee, the hearing focuses on three main areas related to TikTok: its consumer privacy and data security practices, its impact on children, and its relationship with the Chinese Communist Party (CCP).

    The hearing aims to shed light on these issues and determine whether or not the app poses a threat to national security.

    National Security Concerns

    Several national security experts have raised concerns that TikTok, which has approximately 150 million American users, could be used as a tool for spying on Americans or for propaganda purposes by the Chinese government.

    Advocates for banning the app in the U.S. argue that TikTok’s parent company, ByteDance, based in China, could be compelled to share user data with the CCP.

    TikTok has already been banned on federal government devices, including military devices. The move came after the U.S. Army and Navy deemed the app a cybersecurity threat.
